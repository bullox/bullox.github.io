I received an email from a student of mine expressing displeasure at the University's choice to share a video of two of our academics discussing the conflict in Catalonia and Spain. I anticipate having my reply on hand will be useful for future student advice, so am posting here. This was my initial response, but I hope that I get further opportunity to engage!

> Thank you for sending this to me. I confess that I am more ignorant than I would like to be on the Catalonia issue. Whilst making the disclaimer that I am not informed enough to comment on this specific issue, I'd like to give you (and the Society, if you'd like to pass this on) some guidance on addressing the matter. I hope that my currently neutral stance on the matter does not prevent you from engaging with what I hope comes across as some fairly constructive, specific and strategic advice -- in fact, I hope that by following this you can help me understand your viewpoints and even change my and others' minds.
>
> Here goes...
>
> There is a strong principle in universities across the world of '[academic freedom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academic_freedom)' -- in this instance there's a [quote](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evelyn_Beatrice_Hall) that sums it up quite nicely: "I disapprove of what you say, but I will defend to the death your right to say it". Therefore my default position, regardless of the message (barring some fairly clearly legally-defined limitations on '[hate speech](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hate_speech_laws_in_the_United_Kingdom)'), would be that it is within the academics' right to voice their views, and for anyone who wishes to challenge them to voice theirs too. It is up to individuals whether to listen!
>
> I would encourage you and your colleagues to engage, as you are doing, by publishing your own thoughts on the matter. Thus a series of thoughts and advice:
>
> One point that I will make (again as an uneducated viewer) is that the video appears to be presented in a measured way, with a large number of what appear to be statements of fact, and some predictions and statements of opinion. There are also statements that seem to represent both sides of the dispute.
>
> Your email, and the statement on the Spanish Society's Facebook page, are clear about your feelings, but unclear as to the details that you disagree with, and the source of your displeasure. This is obviously a very emotionally charged issue, but I recommend disputing with a rational and considered argument. I recommend that you work as a group to spread the not-inconsiderable work required to disagree at an intellectual level, and publish a counter-argument in text or video format:
>
> * Detail the facts that you disagree with, the reasons that you feel that the video presents a "blatantly one sided and narrow view" and a "completely biased opinion", and additional (ideally referenced) facts that you feel would need to be added to balance out the alleged bias.
> * Help readers understand your disappointment and disgust by adding (clearly-labeled) personal opinion, and/or (ideally referenced/quoted) opinion statements and/or (again sourced) quantitative data on wider opinion.
> * Be extremely careful when labelling statements as 'hate' and 'discrimination' - as I said above these are very specific words, charged with emotion, and if you use them inappropriately it will undermine your arguments.
> * Unless you can identify reasons that this video meets criteria for those categories, address with reasoned and well thought-out public debate.
>
> The 'academic freedom' argument above, which is well-recognised as separating individual from institutional opinion, applies here, but you are rational to question the University's bias/stance in publishing this video on social media. I advise that you modify your approach and request that the University recognise and share your counter-arguments -- use [the University's own strategy statements](http://www.bristol.ac.uk/university/strategy/) to support your case. Our educational strategy includes emphasis on "Global Citizenship", the research section states that we have a "commitment to critical enquiry" and the international goals include a desire to "internationalise the student experience" and "enhance the intercultural understanding and sensitivity of our entire community".
>
> I hope you understand my stance on this -- I am aware that I do not understand the full facts of the politics and opinions but am eager to hear more, and I hope that the above is useful in structuring your approach.
